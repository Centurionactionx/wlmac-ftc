# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

## Users

Prospective sponsors evaluating whether to back WLMAC Robotics — the student-run FIRST Tech Challenge program at William Lyon Mackenzie CI in North York, Toronto (teams CyberLyons #27964 and MechLyons #32514). Three audiences, in priority order:

1. **Local businesses** — North York shops, restaurants, and family-owned businesses near the school looking for community goodwill and local visibility.
2. **Tech / engineering firms** — STEM-aligned companies that care about building the talent pipeline and want their brand associated with young engineers.
3. **Large corporate sponsors** — regional or national brands with real marketing budgets who can take a flagship (Lyon Champion) tier.

Context: a decision-maker skimming a sponsorship packet, likely on a phone or shared over email, deciding in minutes whether this program is worth a few hundred to a few thousand dollars. The job to be done is "quickly judge that this is a real, well-run program and understand exactly what my money buys."

## Product Purpose

A single-page sponsorship packet that converts interest into booked partnerships for the 2026/27 season. It exists because the program runs on ~$10,000 CAD/season across two teams and is funded by sponsors, not the school. Success = partners reaching out and committing to a tier (Cub / Challenger / Lyon Champion). The page must make a young, two-seasons-in program read as credible and worth backing early.

## Brand Personality

**Scrappy but credible.** Student-built and honest about being early — but unmistakably serious engineers, not hobbyists. Voice is plain-spoken, specific, and confident without overclaiming: "Built from scratch," real team numbers, a line-item budget, actual outreach counts. It treats sponsorship as a partnership with a clear value exchange, never a charity ask. Proud of the underdog story, never apologetic about it.

## Anti-references

- **Loud, gimmicky startup.** No gradient hype, buzzword soup, over-animation, or SaaS-landing theatrics. Confidence comes from specifics, not volume.
- **Amateur school-club page.** No cramped spacing, inconsistent type, clip-art, or rushed-student-project feel. The craft itself is proof the team is capable.
- (Implicit) the generic styleshout/agency template it was built from — every section should read as intentional to this program, not as untouched theme scaffolding.

## Design Principles

1. **Proof over promises.** Real teams, real budget line items, real outreach numbers do the persuading. Show the work; don't assert quality.
2. **Partnership, not charity.** Frame every ask as a value exchange (logo on the robot, campus reach, sponsor showcase), never a donation plea.
3. **Own the early-stage story.** "Young program on the rise" is an asset — get in before it's obvious. Never hedge or apologize for being two seasons in.
4. **Craft is the credential.** For an engineering team, a precise, polished page is itself evidence the team can build. Sloppiness would undercut the pitch.
5. **Respect the sponsor's minute.** Clear tiers, a clear ask, and an obvious next step ("Become a Partner"). Don't make a busy decision-maker dig.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target **WCAG 2.1 AA**. On the dark-navy (`#0D1B2A`) surface, hold body text to ≥4.5:1 and large text to ≥3:1 — watch gold-on-navy and muted-gray-on-navy pairings especially. Full keyboard navigation for the nav, buttons, and gallery. Honor `prefers-reduced-motion` for the AOS scroll reveals, slider, and preloader (crossfade or instant fallback). Provide meaningful `alt` text on team/robot photography.
